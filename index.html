About Sordish

Overview

Sordish is characterized by flowing consonants and melodic vowel combinations. The language emphasizes harmony with nature and spiritual connection.

History

The Sordish language traces its origins back to the East Merkopan tribes, evolving over centuries into a refined cultural identity.

Grammar

Sordish follows a grammar close to that of English and German, and is a fusional language. It has three grammatical genders.

Noun inflection

Masculine

Start with a vowel
End in -ium or -ng

Singular: alem (man)

Plural: alemes (men)

Feminine

Start with a consonant
End in a vowel

Singular: waime (woman)

Plural: waimes (women)

Neuter

All remaining nouns

Singular: prosdenten (president)

Plural: prosdentene (presidents)

Possessivity is expressed with the suffix -s, as in mastium "master", mastiums "master's".

Verb Inflection

Verbs are conjugated based on their type (I-VII), determined by their ending. Click the "declension" button on any verb in the dictionary to see its full conjugation.

Syntax

Word Order

Sordish follows a word order similar to English: Subject – Verb – Object.

Questions

Questions in Sordish can be formed by placing an interrogative word at the beginning of the sentence, or by using subject–auxiliary inversion.

Definite Articles and Definiteness

Sordish has a definite article for each grammatical gender. When a noun is definite, it is capitalized. Neutral and feminine articles may also be attached to the noun using an apostrophe.

  • Ely morgna wes core — “And morning will come”
  • Ely a Morgna wes core — “And the morning will come”
  • Ely a’Morgna wes core — “And the morning will come”

Phonological Notes

  • <c> is pronounced as /ts/.
  • <ng> is pronounced as /ŋ/.
  • <nk> may be pronounced as /ŋk/ or /nk/.
  • <sk> may be pronounced as /ʃk/ in more rural accents.
  • <kw> is pronounced as /kʷ/, and <gw> as /gʷ/.
  • <q> is pronounced as /kʷ/ at the start and end of a word, but as /gʷ/ in voiced environments.
  • <z> is pronounced as /dz/, but may be /z/ in Southern accents.
  • <f> may be pronounced as /ɸʷ/ in older dialects and /fʷ/ in newer ones.
  • <t>, <d>, and <s> are dental or retracted dental.
